Harira: Morocco's Soul in a Bowl

A centuries-old tomato and lentil soup that breaks the Ramadan fast and warms Moroccan winters alike — fragrant with saffron, lemon, and handfuls of fresh herb.

The Story Behind the Pot

Harira is not merely a soup. It is a ritual — the bowl that Moroccan families set on the table at the sound of the Maghrib call to prayer each evening during Ramadan. Its amber colour glows against the lamplight, its steam carries the perfume of saffron and preserved lemon, and its thickening agent, a mixture of flour and water called tdouira, gives it a silkiness that no other broth quite replicates.

The word harira comes from the Arabic for silk, and when you stir a proper pot of it, you understand the name entirely.

Method

  1. Build the flavour base. Warm the olive oil in a heavy-bottomed pot over medium heat. Add the onion and celery and cook, stirring, for about 8 minutes until softened and beginning to turn golden at the edges. Add the garlic and cook one minute more.
  2. Brown the meat. Add the lamb and cook, turning occasionally, until lightly browned on most sides, 5–6 minutes. Don't rush this step — the caramelisation goes into the final flavour.
  3. Build the broth. Stir in the crushed tomatoes, tomato paste, all the spices, and the saffron in its soaking water. Pour in the stock, add the lentils, and bring to a gentle boil. Reduce heat, cover partially, and simmer for 30 minutes until the lentils are nearly tender.
  4. Add the chickpeas. Stir in the drained chickpeas and continue to simmer, uncovered, for another 15 minutes.
  5. Thicken with the tdouira. Whisk the flour and cold water together until perfectly smooth. Slowly pour this slurry into the simmering soup, stirring constantly. The soup will thicken noticeably within a few minutes.
  6. Add the noodles and herbs. Add the broken vermicelli and cook for 4–5 minutes until tender. Remove from heat and stir in the lemon juice, parsley, and coriander. Taste and adjust salt.
  7. Serve. Ladle into deep bowls with extra lemon wedges, a drizzle of good olive oil, and a plate of medjool dates — the traditional accompaniment.

Harira keeps well in the fridge for up to three days and, like most soups of this kind, tastes better on the second day. Reheat gently and add a splash of water if it has thickened too much.
